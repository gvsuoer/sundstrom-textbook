For the inductive step, let \(k \in \mathbb{N}\) with \(k \geq 2\text{.}\) We will assume that \(P( 2 ), P( 3 ), \ldots , P( k )\) are true. The goal now is to prove that \(P( {k + 1} )\) is true. To prove this, we assume that \((k + 1)\) has two prime factorizations and then prove that these prime factorizations are the same. So we assume that

\(k + 1 = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_r\) and that \(k + 1 = q_1 q_2 \cdots q_s\text{,}\) where \(p_1 , p_2 , \ldots, p_r\) and \(q_1 , q_2 , \ldots, q_s\) are primes with \(p_1 \leq p_2 \leq \cdots \leq p_r\) and \(q_1 \leq q_2 \leq \cdots \leq q_s\text{.}\)
We must now prove that \(r = s\text{,}\) and for each \(j\) from 1 to \(r\text{,}\) \(p_j = q_j\text{.}\) We can break our proof into two cases: (1) \(p_1 \leq q_1\text{;}\) and (2) \(q_1 \leq p_1\text{.}\) Since one of these must be true, and since the proofs will be similar, we can assume, without loss of generality, that \(p_1 \leq q_1\text{.}\)
