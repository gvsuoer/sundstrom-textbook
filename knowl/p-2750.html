Notice that if \(A = \emptyset\text{,}\) then the conditional statement, “For each \(x \in U\text{,}\) if \(x \in \emptyset\text{,}\) then \(x \in B\)” must be true since the hypothesis will always be false. Another way to look at this is to consider the following statement:

\(\emptyset \not \subseteq B\) means that there exists an \(x \in \emptyset\) such that \(x \notin B\text{.}\)
However, this statement must be false since there does not exist an \(x\) in \(\emptyset\text{.}\) Since this is false, we must conclude that \(\emptyset \subseteq B\text{.}\) Although the facts that \(\emptyset \subseteq B\) and \(B \subseteq B\) may not seem very important, we will use these facts later, and hence we summarize them in Theorem 5.3.
